About the Project

A Plaza Designed for Comfort, Mental Wellness, and Belonging

Montefiore Square sits at the intersection of Broadway and West 138th Street in Hamilton Heights, a dense Harlem neighborhood where public space carries real social and health value. Today the plaza is dominated by hard surfaces, minimal shade, and limited seating — conditions that make the space feel harsh rather than restorative.

This redesign reframes the plaza as wellness infrastructure. Instead of treating it as a simple amenity, the proposal asks how design can reduce heat stress, support mental restoration, encourage social connection, and create a stronger sense of community belonging for the people who use the site every day.

Essential Question: How can urban plaza design promote physical comfort, mental wellness, and community belonging in a dense city neighborhood?

Green Space + Mental Health

Public health research links regular access to nature with lower stress, better mood, and improved overall wellbeing — making neighborhood plazas especially important in dense communities with limited private outdoor space.

Heat as a Health Issue

Shade is not just a comfort upgrade. In heat-vulnerable neighborhoods, tree canopy and cooling features work as public health interventions that reduce thermal stress and make outdoor space usable for longer periods.

Belonging Through Public Space

Well-maintained plazas can reduce isolation by giving residents a place to rest, gather, and see one another. In a high-density neighborhood, quality public space becomes part of everyday community life.

The Problem

Why the Current Plaza Falls Short

Montefiore Square currently underperforms as a public plaza because it does not support the comfort, accessibility, or restorative qualities that neighborhood residents need. It functions more as a pass-through than a place to stay.

For Hamilton Heights residents — especially seniors, families, and commuters — the lack of shade, greenery, and welcoming seating weakens the plaza's ability to serve as a meaningful daily public space.

🌡️

Heat Exposure

Hard paving stores heat and the minimal tree canopy leaves users exposed during the hottest parts of the day.

🪑

Limited Seating Choice

The plaza lacks flexible seating options that support seniors, families, and longer periods of rest.

🌿

Low Ecological Value

Little planting means less biodiversity, less shade, and fewer sensory qualities that make a place feel calm.

👥

Weak Social Activation

Without wellness-oriented programming or focal gathering points, there is little reason for people to linger.

Site + Community Context

Designing for Hamilton Heights Specifically

Montefiore Square sits in Hamilton Heights, a dense residential section of Harlem with strong Dominican-American, African-American, and West African community presence. Because many residents live in apartments without private outdoor space, the plaza plays an outsized role in everyday comfort, social connection, and access to greenery.

01

Primary Users

Seniors, families, youth, and daily commuters all use the site differently, so the redesign must support both short stops and longer restorative visits.

02

Bilingual Access

English and Spanish signage is part of the design baseline, helping the plaza feel legible and welcoming to the surrounding community.

03

Wellness-Focused Programming

Rather than a performance venue, the selected concept supports lighter programming such as walking groups, planting days, quiet workshops, and neighborhood wellness activities.

Design Options

Three Distinct Directions Were Explored

AI image generation was used to test multiple design directions quickly, but the final concept was selected through human judgment, neighborhood context, feasibility, and alignment with the essential question.

Option 01

Green Canopy Plaza

Prioritized maximum shade and a stronger tree canopy, creating a cooler urban refuge with a heavily planted atmosphere.

Option 02

Wellness Garden

Balanced shade, sensory planting, restorative seating, and a small fountain to support mental restoration and everyday social use.

Option 03

Community Gathering Hub

Focused on activation, larger open areas, and event use, but placed less emphasis on quiet, restorative wellness.

Final Proposal

The Wellness Garden Plaza

The Wellness Garden Plaza was selected because it best addresses the essential question. It provides shade, greenery, and social comfort, but it also creates a more restorative environment for everyday mental wellness — something the other two options did not do as directly.

Design Justification

Specific Features, Real Constraints

The final design is not just greener — it is more specific, more feasible, and more tailored to the site. It focuses on planted shade, restorative seating, bilingual interpretation, and a modest fountain feature while still respecting realistic public-plaza constraints such as ADA circulation, clear paths, durable materials, and manageable maintenance.

The proposal also builds on real NYC precedents such as Westchester Square and Myrtle/Wyckoff Plaza, adapting those lessons to the scale, density, and community needs of Hamilton Heights.

  • Maintain accessible circulation and clear paths through the site.
  • Favor movable seating and modest interventions over oversized permanent structures.
  • Use bilingual signage and wellness-oriented programming rather than event-heavy activation.
🌳

Canopy Trees + Shade

Honeylocust, Red Maple, and Serviceberry provide layered canopy, seasonal interest, and urban-tolerant shade.

These species help cool the plaza while preserving visual openness and year-round character.

🪑

Varied Seating Zones

Approx. 60-70 movable chairs, 15 small tables, fixed benches, and wellness-oriented seating moments near planting beds.

Around 20-25% of seating should be ADA-height with armrests to better serve seniors.

🌸

Seasonal Planting Beds

Purple Coneflower, Black-eyed Susan, native asters, Little Bluestem, and other native species create a four-season sensory landscape.

The planting palette supports biodiversity while making the space feel calmer and more alive.

💧

Recirculating Fountain

A small recirculating fountain adds cooling, ambient sound, and a social focal point without requiring an oversized water intervention.

The fountain can be winterized seasonally, making it more practical for NYC maintenance conditions.

Reflection + Conclusion

What AI Helped With — and What Human Judgment Still Had to Do

AI was used as an exploratory design tool, not as a substitute for site knowledge or judgment. The most important design decisions came from evaluating which ideas actually fit Hamilton Heights, the wellness framing, and the practical realities of a NYC public plaza.

What AI Helped With

  • Generating multiple visual directions quickly from the same site idea
  • Testing atmosphere, planting density, and spatial character before selecting a final concept
  • Producing persuasive before/after visuals that clarified the design narrative
  • Helping compare alternative arrangements without manually drawing each one

What AI Got Wrong

  • Some images felt too polished, too suburban, or too detached from the density of Hamilton Heights
  • Several outputs implied unrealistic features that would be hard to maintain or overbuilt for the site
  • AI could suggest aesthetics, but it could not judge neighborhood appropriateness on its own

Why Wellness Garden Won

  • The Green Canopy Plaza offered strong shade but less restorative programming and sensory variety
  • The Community Gathering Hub supported activation, but not the same level of calm and mental restoration
  • The Wellness Garden best matched the essential question by combining shade, rest, nature, and belonging

“AI was a powerful brainstorming partner — but every meaningful design decision came from understanding the people and the place, not from a prompt.”

Research Support

The proposal now frames wellness as an evidence-based design issue linked to shade, green space, mental restoration, and social connection.

Neighborhood Specificity

The project names Hamilton Heights directly and treats bilingual access, seniors, and density as design drivers rather than background details.

Implementation Logic

Budget, phasing, and success metrics make the proposal read more like a realistic plan and less like a purely visual concept.
